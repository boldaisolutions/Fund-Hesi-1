Chapter 1 – Nursing Today

▼

What Is Nursing?

Nursing is simultaneously a science and an art. As a science, nursing relies on a rigorous body of knowledge derived from biology, physiology, pharmacology, psychology, and sociology. As an art, nursing integrates compassionate, individualized care that responds to the whole person. The American Nurses Association (ANA) defines nursing as the protection, promotion, and optimization of health and abilities; the prevention of illness and injury; the alleviation of suffering; and the advocacy for patients, families, communities, and populations.

⭐
Clinical Pearl – NCLEX PriorityNursing is not merely task-based. NCLEX and HESI questions test your ability to THINK like a nurse — prioritize, advocate, educate, and evaluate outcomes.

Benner's Stages of Nursing Proficiency

Patricia Benner's influential model describes how nurses develop clinical expertise through five progressive stages: Novice (no experience, relies on rules), Advanced Beginner (recognizes recurring patterns, needs guidance), Competent (2–3 years experience, organized and efficient), Proficient (sees situations holistically, adapts fluidly), and Expert (intuitive grasp, deeply experienced). Understanding your current stage helps you identify learning needs and seek appropriate mentorship.

🧠
Memory Trick – "NACLE + Expert"Novice → Advanced Beginner → Competent → proficient → Expert. Think: "Nobody Argues Can Provide Excellence" (N-A-C-P-E).

Professional Nursing Roles

Nurses function in multiple roles simultaneously. As a Caregiver, you provide direct, compassionate patient care. As an Advocate, you protect patient rights and ensure informed decision-making. As an Educator, you teach patients, families, and communities about health management. As a Communicator, you use therapeutic techniques and interprofessional collaboration. As a Manager, you coordinate care, delegate safely, and manage resources efficiently. The role of Researcher drives evidence-based practice forward.

Scope and Standards of Practice

The ANA Scope and Standards of Practice provides the definitive framework for nursing. It defines nursing, establishes professional expectations, guides safe patient care, and includes the Code of Ethics for Nurses. Nurse Practice Acts at the state level legally define scope of practice, protect the public, and regulate licensure. Nurses are accountable to these standards in every clinical encounter.

🚨
Safety AlertPracticing outside your scope of practice creates legal liability and patient harm risk. Always know your state's Nurse Practice Act.

Historical Foundations

Florence Nightingale established the first nursing philosophy centered on health maintenance, environmental manipulation, and organized nursing education. She is recognized as the first practicing epidemiologist. During the Civil War era, nurses such as Clara Barton, Dorothea Dix, Mary Ann Ball ("Mother Bickerdyke"), Harriet Tubman, Mary Mahoney, Isabel Hampton Robb, Lillian Wald, and Mary Brewster shaped the profession's values of service and advocacy. The 20th century brought university-based education, specialization, Magnet recognition, and research-based practice. The 21st century demands readiness for aging populations, cultural diversity, emerging infections, disaster preparedness, bioterrorism, and technology integration.

Current Trends in Nursing

Evidence-Based Practice (EBP) remains the cornerstone of modern nursing. QSEN (Quality and Safety Education for Nurses) competencies — patient-centered care, teamwork, EBP, quality improvement, safety, and informatics — guide curriculum design. Emerging technologies, genomics, telehealth, and artificial intelligence are reshaping the profession. Nurses increasingly influence health policy at local, state, and national levels.

✅
NCLEX TipQSEN competencies are frequently tested. Know all six: Patient-Centered Care, Teamwork & Collaboration, Evidence-Based Practice, Quality Improvement, Safety, and Informatics.

Chapter 6 – Health and Wellness

▼

Defining Health

The World Health Organization (WHO) defines health as "a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being, and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity." Health is dynamic — it exists on a continuum influenced by internal factors (genetics, age, developmental stage, emotions, spirituality) and external factors (culture, environment, education, socioeconomic status, lifestyle choices). Wellness is the active, ongoing process of making choices that enhance health and quality of life.

Models of Health and Illness

Several models guide nursing's understanding of health. The Health Belief Model explains health-related decisions through perceived susceptibility, perceived severity, perceived benefits, perceived barriers, cues to action, and self-efficacy. The Health Promotion Model (Pender) focuses on expanding health potential. Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs guides prioritization — physiological needs must be met before higher-order needs. The Holistic Health Model considers the emotional, spiritual, social, cultural, and physical dimensions of wellness.

Levels of Prevention

Primary Prevention reduces the incidence of disease before it occurs — think vaccinations, health education, and safety counseling. Secondary Prevention focuses on early detection and treatment to prevent disease progression — screenings like mammograms or blood pressure checks. Tertiary Prevention occurs when disease is established and aims to reduce complications and restore maximum function — cardiac rehabilitation or diabetes management programs.

🧠
Memory Trick – 1-2-3 Prevention1° = PREVENT IT. 2° = DETECT IT EARLY. 3° = MANAGE & MINIMIZE COMPLICATIONS.

Illness Behavior and Impact

Illness is a state in which physical, emotional, intellectual, social, developmental, or spiritual functioning is diminished. Acute illness is severe but short-lived; chronic illness lasts more than 6 months and significantly affects daily functioning. Illness creates behavioral and emotional changes, affects body image, alters self-concept, disrupts family roles, and changes family dynamics. Nurses must address these psychosocial dimensions as part of holistic care.

Chapter 22 – Ethics and Values

▼

Core Ethical Principles

Nursing practice is grounded in ethical principles that guide every clinical decision. Autonomy means respecting the patient's right to make informed decisions about their own care. Beneficence requires acting in the patient's best interest. Nonmaleficence demands that nurses "do no harm." Justice ensures fair distribution of resources and treatment. Fidelity involves keeping promises and honoring commitments. Veracity demands truthfulness in communication.

🧠
Memory Trick – "ABN JFV"Autonomy, Beneficence, Nonmaleficence, Justice, Fidelity, Veracity. Remember: "A Big Nurse Just Finds Victories"

Code of Ethics for Nurses (ANA)

The ANA Code of Ethics provides the moral framework for professional nursing. Key provisions emphasize advocacy for patient dignity and rights, responsibility and accountability, confidentiality of patient information, and professional integrity. It guides nurses when facing ethical dilemmas — situations where competing values create uncertainty about the right course of action.

Processing Ethical Problems

When facing an ethical dilemma, nurses follow a structured process: (1) Ask whether the issue is truly ethical. (2) Gather relevant information. (3) Identify the ethical elements and examine your own values. (4) Name the problem. (5) Identify possible courses of action. (6) Create and implement an action plan. (7) Evaluate the outcomes. Ethics committees in healthcare institutions provide additional support for complex dilemmas involving quality of life, end-of-life care, and access to care.

Chapter 23 – Legal Implications in Nursing Practice

▼

Legal Framework for Nursing

Nursing practice is governed by multiple layers of law. Nurse Practice Acts (civil/statutory law) are state-level laws that define scope of practice, establish licensure requirements, and protect the public. Administrative law is enforced by State Boards of Nursing. Common law evolves from court decisions and establishes precedents for nursing care standards.

Key Federal Statutes

HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act) protects the privacy and confidentiality of protected health information (PHI). EMTALA prohibits hospitals from transferring unstabilized patients for financial reasons. The Patient Self-Determination Act requires healthcare institutions to provide patients with information about their rights, including the right to refuse treatment and to complete advance directives. The ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act) protects employees and patients with disabilities.

🚨
HIPAA AlertSharing ANY patient information — even to family members — without the patient's consent violates HIPAA. Always verify what information patients have authorized to be shared.

Negligence and Malpractice

Negligence is conduct that falls below the accepted standard of care of a reasonably prudent person. Malpractice is professional negligence — it requires proving duty, breach of duty, causation, and damages (the four elements). Nurses can be held personally liable for malpractice. Accurate, complete, objective, and timely documentation is both a communication tool and a legal defense.

✅
NCLEX Tip – Informed ConsentThe nurse's role is to WITNESS informed consent, not to obtain it. If a patient expresses doubts after signing, notify the provider IMMEDIATELY before the procedure proceeds.

Chapter 5 – Evidence-Based Practice

▼

What Is Evidence-Based Practice?

Evidence-Based Practice (EBP) is a problem-solving approach to clinical care that integrates the best available research evidence, the clinician's own expertise, and the patient's individual preferences and values. EBP ensures that nursing interventions are grounded in current science rather than tradition or habit. It improves patient safety, reduces variability in care, and drives quality improvement.

The EBP Process (7 Steps)

Step 0: Cultivate a spirit of inquiry — always question current practices. Step 1: Ask a clinical question in PICOT format. Step 2: Search for the best available evidence from peer-reviewed journals, clinical guidelines, and databases. Step 3: Critically appraise the evidence for scientific rigor, relevance, and validity. Step 4: Integrate the evidence with clinical expertise and patient preferences. Step 5: Evaluate the outcomes of the practice change. Step 6: Communicate and disseminate the results. Step 7: Sustain the change by embedding it into culture and policy.

🧠
PICOT FormatP = Patient Population | I = Intervention | C = Comparison | O = Outcome | T = Time
Example: "In hospitalized adult patients (P), does hourly rounding (I) compared to standard call light use (C) reduce fall rates (O) within 30 days (T)?"

Types of Research

Quantitative research uses statistical methods to measure outcomes (experimental/nonexperimental designs). Qualitative research explores lived experiences and perceptions through interviews or observation. The hierarchy of evidence places systematic reviews and meta-analyses at the highest level, followed by randomized controlled trials, cohort studies, case-control studies, and expert opinion at the lowest level.

✅
NCLEX TipThe highest level of evidence is a systematic review or meta-analysis of multiple randomized controlled trials. Expert opinion is the LOWEST level of evidence.

Chapter 16 – Nursing Assessment

▼

Assessment as the Foundation

Nursing assessment is the FIRST step of the nursing process and serves as the foundation for all clinical decisions. It is a continuous, systematic process that collects, validates, organizes, and documents patient data. Without thorough assessment, the nurse cannot accurately identify problems, set priorities, or evaluate outcomes. Assessment drives every subsequent step of the nursing process.

Types of Data

Subjective data is what the patient tells you — symptoms, feelings, perceptions, concerns ("I have chest pain"). Objective data is what you observe, measure, or detect through physical examination and diagnostic testing (BP 180/100, temperature 102.1°F, diaphoretic skin). Both types are essential. Nurses always validate data — for example, confirm that a patient complaining of pain (subjective) also shows a pain behavior such as guarding (objective).

The Health History

A complete nursing health history includes: biographical information, chief complaint/reason for seeking care, present illness or health concerns, past health history, family history, psychosocial history, spiritual health, lifestyle and habits, review of systems, and observation of patient behavior. Conducting the interview requires communication skills, cultural sensitivity, and the ability to recognize both stated and unstated concerns.

Physical Assessment Techniques

The four techniques used in physical assessment are inspection (deliberate, systematic visual examination), palpation (use of touch to assess texture, temperature, moisture, organ location, pulsations), percussion (tapping to produce sounds that reveal density of underlying structures), and auscultation (listening to body sounds with a stethoscope). These techniques are performed in the order IPPA — except for abdominal assessment, where auscultation precedes percussion and palpation to avoid altering bowel sounds.

🚨
Abdominal Exception!For the abdomen: Inspect → Auscultate → Percuss → Palpate (IAPP). Palpation/percussion BEFORE auscultation may alter bowel sounds — a common NCLEX trap!

Clinical Judgment

Clinical judgment uses the NCLEX Next Generation model: Recognize Cues (identify relevant information), Analyze Cues (determine clinical significance), Prioritize Hypotheses (rank problems by urgency), Generate Solutions (identify evidence-based interventions), Take Action (implement the plan), and Evaluate Outcomes (assess effectiveness and reassess).

✅
NGN Clinical Judgment ModelRecognize → Analyze → Prioritize → Generate → Take Action → Evaluate. This framework underlies ALL Next Generation NCLEX questions.

Chapter 24 – Communication

▼

Therapeutic vs. Nontherapeutic Communication

Therapeutic communication is purposeful, patient-centered, and goal-directed. Key techniques include: Active listening (full attention, minimal interruption), Empathy (acknowledging feelings without judgment), Open-ended questions ("Tell me more about…"), Clarification ("I want to make sure I understand — are you saying…?"), Reflection (rephrasing the patient's words), Silence (giving space for thought), Summarization, and Focusing (directing attention to important issues).

Nontherapeutic techniques to AVOID: giving personal opinions, false reassurance ("Everything will be fine"), changing the subject, asking "why" questions (implies judgment), automatic responses, excessive probing, approval/disapproval, arguing, and defensive responses.

🚨
Common NCLEX Trap"Don't worry, I'm sure it will be fine" = FALSE REASSURANCE and nontherapeutic. Instead say: "I can see you're worried. Tell me what concerns you most."

SBAR Communication

SBAR stands for Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation — a structured communication tool that improves clarity and reduces errors during handoffs and provider notifications. Situation: What is happening right now? Background: What is the clinical context? Assessment: What do you think the problem is? Recommendation: What action do you recommend? SBAR reduces ambiguity and is endorsed by The Joint Commission for patient safety.

Chapter 26 – Informatics and Documentation

▼

Principles of Quality Documentation

Documentation is both a clinical tool and a legal record. Quality nursing documentation must be: Factual (objective findings, not interpretations), Accurate (correct information with proper measurements), Complete (all relevant data included), Current (timely entries), Organized (logical flow), and Confidential (HIPAA-compliant). Never document care before providing it, and never document inaccurate information.

🚨
Legal WarningThe medical record is a LEGAL DOCUMENT. Falsifying, altering, or destroying records is illegal. If you forget to document, add a "late entry" note with the current date and time.

Electronic Health Records

The Electronic Health Record (EHR) provides a lifetime, comprehensive digital patient record accessible to all authorized healthcare team members. It differs from the Electronic Medical Record (EMR), which captures a single provider visit. EHRs support clinical decision making through alerts, clinical practice guidelines, and computerized provider order entry (CPOE). Nurses must protect PHI through passwords, firewalls, automatic logouts, and appropriate handling of printed records.

Chapter 27 – Patient Safety and Quality

▼

National Patient Safety Goals (NPSGs)

The Joint Commission's NPSGs provide annual evidence-based standards to prevent common, serious errors. Key goals include: accurately identifying patients (two identifiers), improving communication among caregivers (SBAR, read-back of verbal orders), using medications safely (labeling, anticoagulant safety), preventing infection (hand hygiene, surgical site infection prevention), identifying patient safety risks (fall assessment), and preventing surgical errors (Universal Protocol, time-out).

Fall Prevention

Falls are the most common adverse event in hospitalized patients. Risk factors include advanced age, impaired mobility, altered cognition, polypharmacy, recent fall history, incontinence, and environmental hazards. Prevention strategies: complete a validated risk assessment (Morse Fall Scale) on admission and each shift, implement fall precautions (non-skid socks, bed alarm, call light within reach, frequent rounding), keep environment clear of hazards, and educate patients and families.

✅
Priority Safety InterventionBefore leaving ANY patient's room: lower the bed, lock wheels, raise appropriate side rails, ensure call light is within reach. These FOUR actions prevent falls and are always correct on NCLEX.

Restraints

Restraints are used ONLY as a last resort when less restrictive measures have failed and there is immediate risk of patient harm. Nurses must: try all alternatives first (reorientation, increased monitoring, family presence), obtain a provider order, explain to the patient and family, document rationale and alternatives tried, perform neurovascular checks every 2 hours, remove restraints at least every 2 hours for range of motion, assess skin integrity, and document continuously.

🚨
Restraint PriorityNEVER tie restraints to side rails. Always tie to the bed FRAME using quick-release knots. This is a patient safety priority tested frequently on HESI.

Chapter 28 – Infection Prevention and Control

▼

Chain of Infection

Infection spreads through a predictable chain of six links. Infectious Agent (bacteria, virus, fungi, parasite). Reservoir (the source where the organism lives — humans, animals, soil, equipment). Portal of Exit (how it leaves the reservoir — respiratory secretions, blood, feces). Mode of Transmission (contact, droplet, airborne, vehicle, vector). Portal of Entry (how it enters a new host — mucous membranes, broken skin, respiratory tract). Susceptible Host (the new victim — immunocompromised patients are at highest risk). Breaking ANY link stops infection.

🧠
Chain of Infection Mnemonic"I Really Place My Protective Shield" = Infectious Agent → Reservoir → Portal of Exit → Mode of Transmission → Portal of Entry → Susceptible Host

Standard Precautions

Standard precautions apply to ALL patients regardless of diagnosis and treat ALL blood, body fluids (except sweat), non-intact skin, and mucous membranes as potentially infectious. Components include: Hand hygiene (most important — use soap and water for C. diff; alcohol-based gel for most pathogens), Gloves (before contact with blood/body fluids), Gown (when splashing is anticipated), Mask and eye protection (if splash risk to face), and Safe injection practices.

Transmission-Based Precautions

Airborne Precautions: Droplets <5 microns that remain suspended — TB, measles, varicella, disseminated zoster. Requires N95 respirator, negative pressure room, keep door closed. Droplet Precautions: Droplets >5 microns — influenza, COVID-19, pertussis, RSV, meningitis. Requires surgical mask, private room preferred. Contact Precautions: Direct or indirect contact — MRSA, VRE, C. diff, wounds. Requires gloves and gown upon entering room.

✅
PPE Order Matters!DONNING (putting on): Gown → Mask/Respirator → Goggles → Gloves. DOFFING (removing): Gloves → Goggles → Gown → Mask. Remember: "Gloves come off FIRST when doffing" — they're the most contaminated.

Chapter 31 – Medication Administration

▼

The Rights of Medication Administration

Safe medication administration requires adherence to the Rights: Right Patient (two identifiers — name + DOB or MRN), Right Medication (verify generic and trade names), Right Dose (calculate accurately), Right Route (oral, IV, IM, SQ, topical), Right Time (within 30 minutes before or after scheduled time), Right Documentation (document immediately after administration, not before), and Right Indication (verify the medication matches the diagnosis).

🧠
Extended RightsSome sources include Right to Refuse, Right Education, Right Assessment, and Right Evaluation. Always assess the patient BEFORE and AFTER medication administration.

Pharmacokinetics

Absorption: Movement from administration site into bloodstream — affected by route, blood flow, lipid solubility, and surface area. IV route = fastest. Distribution: Movement through blood to tissues — affected by protein binding (only free drug is active), membrane permeability, and circulation. Metabolism (Biotransformation): Primarily in the liver. First-pass effect significantly reduces oral drug bioavailability. Excretion: Primarily through kidneys. Renal impairment requires dose adjustments.

🚨
Renal & Hepatic ConsiderationsPatients with kidney disease accumulate drugs → toxicity risk. Patients with liver disease have impaired metabolism → toxicity risk. Always assess renal and hepatic function before medication administration.

Types of Medication Effects

Therapeutic effect: The intended, desired response. Side effect: A predictable, secondary effect (drowsiness with antihistamines). Adverse effect: An unintended, harmful reaction requiring intervention. Toxic effect: Dangerous accumulation — monitor drug levels, renal/hepatic function. Idiosyncratic reaction: Unpredictable, unusual response. Allergic reaction: Immune-mediated response — ranges from urticaria to anaphylaxis. Drug interaction: Two drugs affecting each other's action (additive, synergistic, antagonistic).

Chapter 32 – Complementary, Alternative, and Integrative Therapies

▼

Definitions and Categories

Complementary therapies are used alongside conventional medical treatment (massage + chemotherapy). Alternative therapies replace conventional treatment (using only herbal remedies instead of prescribed medication). Integrative healthcare combines evidence-based complementary therapies with conventional care, treating the whole person. Common therapies include massage, meditation, guided imagery, biofeedback, acupuncture, Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), chiropractic therapy, yoga, aromatherapy, herbal products, and music therapy.

Nursing Responsibilities

Nurses must routinely assess patients for use of all complementary therapies, including herbal supplements, because patients often do not volunteer this information. Many herbal products interact with prescribed medications — for example, St. John's Wort reduces the effectiveness of warfarin and oral contraceptives; ginkgo biloba increases bleeding risk. Nurses educate patients about safe use, potential interactions, and the importance of disclosing all therapies to their healthcare team. Cultural respect and patient autonomy guide this conversation.

🚨
Critical Herb-Drug InteractionsSt. John's Wort → ↓ warfarin, contraceptives, digoxin. Ginkgo/Garlic/Ginger/Fish Oil → ↑ bleeding risk. Kava → hepatotoxicity. Always ask about herbal supplements!

Chapter 40 – Hygiene

▼

Hygiene as Nursing Care

Personal hygiene promotes health, comfort, dignity, and infection prevention. It includes bathing, oral care, hair care, nail care, skin care, eye/ear/nose care, and perineal care. Hygiene care provides valuable assessment opportunities — the nurse observes skin condition, mobility, cognition, and psychosocial well-being during bathing. Factors influencing hygiene practices include age, culture, health status, mobility, socioeconomic status, personal preferences, and body image.

Oral Care Priorities

Oral hygiene prevents dental caries, gingivitis, parotitis (salivary gland infection), and aspiration pneumonia. For unconscious patients, oral care with suction every 2–4 hours prevents ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP) — one of the most critical nursing interventions in the ICU. Position unconscious patients on their side during oral care to prevent aspiration.

🚨
Oral Care for Ventilated PatientsChlorhexidine oral rinse + elevated HOB (30–45°) + oral suctioning = VAP Bundle. These interventions significantly reduce ventilator-associated pneumonia risk.

Chapter 9 – Cultural Competence

▼

Campinha-Bacote's Model

Cultural competence requires five components: Cultural Awareness (self-examination of your own biases), Cultural Knowledge (learning about other cultures' health beliefs, values, disease prevalence, and treatment efficacy), Cultural Skill (ability to conduct a culturally sensitive assessment), Cultural Encounters (direct cross-cultural interactions that modify existing beliefs), and Cultural Desire (genuine motivation to become culturally competent).

Health Disparities and Social Determinants

Health disparities are differences in health outcomes closely linked to social, economic, and environmental disadvantage. Social determinants of health — the conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age — profoundly influence health. Marginalized groups experience higher rates of chronic disease, reduced access to care, and earlier death. Nurses address disparities through culturally competent care, use of qualified medical interpreters, plain language education, and advocacy for equitable healthcare access.

🚨
Interpreter AlertNEVER use minor children as medical interpreters. Always use a qualified, trained medical interpreter — either in person or via video/phone interpretation services. Using family members can result in privacy violations and inaccurate information.

Chapter 45 – Nutrition

▼

Essential Nutrients

The six essential nutrients are carbohydrates, proteins, fats, water, vitamins, and minerals. Carbohydrates are the body's primary energy source (4 kcal/g). Proteins are essential for tissue repair, immune function, and enzyme production (4 kcal/g). Fats provide concentrated energy, fat-soluble vitamin absorption, and cell membrane integrity (9 kcal/g). Water maintains fluid balance and is involved in every metabolic process. Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) are stored in body fat; water-soluble vitamins (B-complex, C) must be replenished regularly.

🧠
Fat-Soluble Vitamins: "ADEK"A = vision, immune function. D = calcium absorption, bone health. E = antioxidant. K = clotting (warfarin patients — monitor K intake!). Fat-soluble vitamins can accumulate to TOXIC levels — water-soluble vitamins are excreted in urine.

Nutritional Assessment

A complete nutritional assessment includes: dietary history (24-hour food recall, food frequency questionnaire), anthropometric measurements (height, weight, BMI, waist circumference, skin fold thickness), laboratory data (albumin half-life 20 days — chronic status; prealbumin half-life 2–3 days — recent status; transferrin, hemoglobin/hematocrit, blood glucose, lipid panel), and physical examination findings (hair loss, pale mucous membranes, skin changes, muscle wasting, fatigue, poor wound healing).

Enteral vs. Parenteral Nutrition

Enteral nutrition (tube feeding) is preferred when the GI tract is functioning — it maintains gut integrity, prevents bacterial translocation, and is safer and less expensive than parenteral nutrition. Routes include nasogastric (NG), nasoduodenal (ND), nasojejunal (NJ), percutaneous endoscopic gastrostomy (PEG), and jejunostomy tubes. Parenteral nutrition (IV nutrition) is used when the GI tract cannot be used — post-GI surgery, severe GI disease, bowel obstruction, short bowel syndrome. Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN) requires central venous access.

✅
Enteral Feeding SafetyALWAYS verify tube placement before each feeding (pH of gastric aspirate <5, or X-ray). Elevate HOB 30–45° during feeding and for 1 hour after. Check gastric residual per facility policy. NEVER assume placement without verification.

Chapter 29 – Vital Signs

▼

Guidelines for Measuring Vital Signs

Vital sign measurement is the nurse's responsibility — never delegate without first assessing the patient's stability. Clean all devices between patients to prevent cross-contamination. Select equipment appropriate for the patient (correct cuff size, thermometer site). Know the patient's baseline and current health history, medications, and therapies. Control environmental factors (activity, temperature, anxiety) before measuring. Analyze trends rather than isolated readings, and communicate significant changes to the provider promptly.

Body Temperature: Physiology and Alterations

Body temperature equals heat produced minus heat lost, regulated by the hypothalamus through neural, vascular, and behavioral mechanisms. Factors that affect temperature include age (infants and older adults are most vulnerable to temperature extremes), exercise, hormone levels (progesterone raises temperature), circadian rhythm (lowest at 0100–0400, highest at 1600–1800), stress, and environment. Fever (>100.4°F / 38°C) is a controlled rise driven by pyrogens. Hyperthermia is an uncontrolled rise that exceeds the body's ability to compensate — heatstroke is a life-threatening form. Hypothermia (<95°F / 35°C) results from prolonged cold exposure and causes metabolic slowing, dysrhythmias, and death if severe.

🧠
Temperature Sites — Know the AdjustmentsRectal = most accurate (gold standard); +0.5–1°F above oral. Axillary = least accurate; −0.5–1°F below oral. Oral: wait 30 min after eating/smoking/drinking. Tympanic: affected by cerumen; avoid with ear infections. Temporal artery: sweep forehead; non-invasive and accurate.
🚨
Rectal ContraindicationsAvoid rectal temperature in: neutropenic patients, rectal surgery or trauma, patients with cardiac disease (vagal stimulation risk), and diarrhea. Use tympanic or temporal artery instead.

Pulse: Physiology and Assessment

The pulse is the palpable bounding of blood flow in a peripheral artery, reflecting the heart's stroke volume and rate. Electrical impulses from the sinoatrial (SA) node trigger ventricular contraction. Assess pulse for rate (beats per minute), rhythm (regular or irregular), strength/amplitude (0 = absent, 1+ = weak/thready, 2+ = normal, 3+ = full/bounding), and equality (compare bilateral pulses). For irregular rhythms, count the apical pulse at the 5th intercostal space, midclavicular line for a full 60 seconds. The pulse deficit = apical rate minus radial rate, measured simultaneously with two nurses; a deficit indicates ineffective ventricular contractions (common in atrial fibrillation).

✅
Nursing Diagnoses for Pulse AlterationsActivity Intolerance, Dehydration, Hypervolemia, Impaired Cardiac Function, Impaired Peripheral Tissue Perfusion. Always correlate pulse findings with BP, SpO2, and patient symptoms.

Respirations: Ventilation, Diffusion, and Perfusion

Respiration involves three processes: Ventilation (mechanical movement of air in/out of lungs), Diffusion (gas exchange across the alveolar-capillary membrane), and Perfusion (blood flow through the pulmonary circulation). Breathing is regulated primarily by CO₂ levels detected by central chemoreceptors. Assess respiratory rate (12–20/min normal), depth (shallow, normal, deep), and rhythm (regular/irregular patterns). Pulse oximetry measures arterial oxygen saturation (SpO₂); capnography monitors end-tidal CO₂. Always count respirations without informing the patient — awareness alters the rate.

Blood Pressure: Physiology and Measurement

Arterial blood pressure is determined by cardiac output (HR × stroke volume), peripheral vascular resistance, blood volume, blood viscosity, and vessel elasticity. Factors increasing BP: age, stress, ethnicity/genetics (African Americans have higher rates of hypertension), obesity, smoking, activity. BP classification: Normal <120/<80; Elevated 120–129/<80; Stage 1 HTN 130–139/80–89; Stage 2 HTN ≥140/≥90; Hypertensive crisis ≥180/≥120. Korotkoff sounds guide auscultatory measurement. Orthostatic hypotension = drop of ≥20 mmHg systolic or ≥10 mmHg diastolic when moving from lying to standing — a fall risk.

🚨
Hypertensive Emergency vs. UrgencyEmergency = BP ≥180/120 WITH end-organ damage (headache, chest pain, vision changes, AMS, renal failure). Notify provider STAT. Urgency = severely elevated BP WITHOUT end-organ damage — urgent but not immediately life-threatening. Always assess for symptoms.
⭐
BP Cuff Size MattersA cuff that is too SMALL gives a falsely HIGH reading. A cuff too LARGE gives a falsely LOW reading. The cuff bladder should encircle 80% of the arm's circumference. This is a frequently tested NCLEX equipment principle.

Oxygen Saturation and Pain as the 5th Vital Sign

Pulse oximetry (SpO₂) provides non-invasive, continuous monitoring of oxygen saturation. Normal: 95–100%. Below 90% = critical hypoxemia requiring immediate intervention. Accuracy is affected by poor perfusion, nail polish, dark pigmentation, hypothermia, carbon monoxide poisoning (falsely HIGH), and patient movement. Rotate SpO₂ probe sites every 2–4 hours to prevent skin breakdown. Pain is the 5th vital sign — it must be assessed at every vital sign measurement using a standardized scale (Numeric, FACES, FLACC), documented, treated, and reassessed to evaluate intervention effectiveness.

Chapter 38 – Activity and Exercise

▼

Benefits of Physical Activity

Regular physical activity elevates mood and attitude, enables physical fitness, helps with smoking cessation, boosts energy, manages stress, promotes better sleep quality, and improves self-image and confidence. For patients with chronic illness, targeted exercise programs improve outcomes in coronary artery disease, hypertension, COPD, and diabetes mellitus. Nurses play a key role in assessing activity tolerance and motivating patients toward movement at every stage of care.

Scientific Knowledge Base: Body Mechanics and Movement

Safe movement depends on body alignment (proper positioning of body segments), balance (center of gravity over base of support), and understanding of gravity and friction. Unsteady patients fall when their center of gravity shifts outside their base of support — a principle that guides fall prevention. The skeletal system (bones, joints, ligaments, tendons, cartilage) provides structure and leverage. Skeletal muscle produces both movement and postural stability. The nervous system, through proprioception and balance mechanisms, coordinates all movement. Pathological influences — congenital defects, bone/joint disorders, CNS disease, musculoskeletal trauma, and obesity — can impair all of these systems.

Safe Patient Handling and Mobility (SPHM)

Safe Patient Handling and Mobility principles protect both patients and nurses from musculoskeletal injury. Before any patient transfer, the nurse must: know the patient's level of mobility, assess risk for activity intolerance, mentally review transfer steps, determine equipment needed (transfer belt, mechanical lift, slide board), position on the patient's unaffected side, and arrange environment to allow adequate space. Using proper body mechanics — wide base of support, low center of gravity, pivoting rather than twisting — reduces injury risk for nurses during all transfers.

🚨
Transfer Safety RulesStand on the patient's STRONGER (unaffected) side. Lock wheelchair wheels before transferring. Never pull from arms or shoulders — use a transfer belt. Raise bed to working height. Two-nurse assist or mechanical lift when patient cannot bear weight.

Nursing Process: Activity and Exercise

Assessment includes readiness to exercise, cultural and socioeconomic factors, body alignment, mobility (sitting, standing, range of motion), gait, exercise pattern, activity tolerance, and environmental factors. Nursing diagnoses: Activity Intolerance, Risk for Fall-Related Injury, Impaired Mobility in Bed, Impaired Mobility, Acute or Chronic Pain. Interventions include early mobility programs (proven to reduce hospital complications), isotonic exercises (muscle contraction with joint movement — walking, resistance training), isometric exercises (muscle contraction without joint movement — quadriceps sets), range-of-motion exercises (active, active-assistive, passive), and walking programs. Assistive devices include walkers, canes (held on the stronger side), and crutches.

✅
Cane Use — NCLEX TipThe cane is held on the STRONGER (unaffected) side. Move the cane and the weak leg forward together, then advance the strong leg. This provides a wider base of support on the weaker side. Common NCLEX question!
🧠
Crutch Gaits4-Point: most stable, partial weight-bearing on both legs. 3-Point: non-weight-bearing on one leg. 2-Point: faster, partial weight-bearing. Swing-to / Swing-through: for paraplegia, full upper extremity strength required.

Chapter 39 – Immobility

▼

Effects of Immobility on Body Systems

Prolonged immobility causes systemic complications across every body system. Metabolic: decreased metabolic rate, negative nitrogen balance (muscle catabolism), anorexia, negative calcium balance (bone demineralization → osteoporosis risk). Respiratory: decreased tidal volume, retained secretions, atelectasis, pneumonia risk — pooling of secretions in the posterior lung bases is a primary concern. Cardiovascular: increased cardiac workload, orthostatic hypotension (blood pools in lower extremities), deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism from venous stasis. Musculoskeletal: muscle atrophy (disuse), joint contractures, foot drop (plantar flexion contracture). Urinary: urinary stasis → UTI, renal calculi (calcium deposits). Integumentary: pressure injuries from sustained pressure, friction, shear, and moisture. Psychosocial: depression, anxiety, social isolation, altered sleep patterns, loss of independence.

🧠
Immobility Complications Mnemonic — "Mr. RUMC"Metabolic, Respiratory, Urinary, Musculoskeletal, Cardiovascular, Integumentary + Psychosocial. Every system affected — assess ALL of them!

Pathological Influences on Mobility

Conditions that impair mobility include postural abnormalities (scoliosis, kyphosis, lordosis), muscle abnormalities (muscular dystrophy, contractures), central nervous system damage (stroke, spinal cord injury, TBI, Parkinson's disease), direct musculoskeletal trauma (fractures, dislocations), and joint disease (osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis). Developmental factors also apply — immobility affects all age groups differently, with infants at risk for developmental delay and older adults at highest risk for falls, fractures, and rapid deconditioning.

Nursing Interventions for the Immobile Patient

Respiratory: turn, cough, and deep breathe every 2 hours; incentive spirometry; early ambulation. Cardiovascular: compression stockings/sequential compression devices (SCDs) for DVT prophylaxis; elevate legs; early ambulation; monitor for orthostatic hypotension when transferring. Musculoskeletal: range-of-motion exercises every 4–8 hours; positioning to prevent contractures; foot board or high-top sneakers to prevent foot drop. Skin: reposition every 2 hours; pressure redistribution surfaces; moisture management; nutrition optimization. Urinary: adequate fluid intake (2–3 L/day if not contraindicated); monitor intake and output; assess for UTI signs. Psychosocial: encourage social interaction; promote independence; provide diversional activities; involve family.

🚨
DVT Warning SignsUnilateral calf warmth, redness, swelling, tenderness, and a positive Homans' sign (pain on dorsiflexion — note: not definitive alone). Report IMMEDIATELY — DVT can progress to pulmonary embolism. Do NOT massage the affected leg.
✅
Positioning Techniques from the SlidesSupported Fowler's: HOB 45–60°, support arms. Supine: flat, pillow under head, trochanter rolls. Prone: face down — contraindicated post-op abdominal surgery. Side-lying/lateral: 30–45° tilt, pillow between knees. Sims (lateral recumbent): semi-prone, used for enemas and unconscious patients.

Chapter 46 – Urinary Elimination

▼

Scientific Knowledge Base: The Urinary System

The urinary system consists of two kidneys (filter ~180 L blood/day, produce urine), two ureters (transport urine via peristalsis), the bladder (storage — normal capacity 300–500 mL; urge to void at ~150–200 mL), and the urethra (outlet; female 3–4 cm, male 20 cm — explains why females have higher UTI rates). Urination is a reflex coordinated by the brainstem and voluntarily controlled by the cerebral cortex. Factors influencing urination include age, food and fluid intake, psychological state, medications (diuretics, anticholinergics, opioids), activity, and disease processes.

Common Urinary Elimination Problems

Urinary retention: inability to empty the bladder despite urge — causes include BPH, post-operative effects, neurogenic bladder, pain, and medications. Signs: distended bladder, lower abdominal discomfort, small frequent voiding (overflow incontinence), absence of voiding. UTI: infection of the urinary tract — signs include dysuria, frequency, urgency, hematuria, cloudy/foul-smelling urine, suprapubic tenderness, fever. CAUTI (catheter-associated UTI) is a nursing-sensitive quality indicator. Urinary incontinence types: Stress (leakage with increased abdominal pressure — cough, sneeze, laugh), Urge (strong, sudden urge with inability to delay), Overflow (from retention), Reflex (no awareness of need), Functional (inability to reach toilet in time). Urinary diversions: Ileal conduit (urostomy), continent urinary reservoir.

🚨
CAUTI Prevention Bundle(1) Insert only when necessary with strict aseptic technique. (2) Use smallest appropriate catheter. (3) Maintain closed drainage system. (4) Keep drainage bag below bladder level at all times. (5) Remove catheter as soon as no longer needed. (6) Daily perineal catheter care. This is a CMS Never Event — preventable with nursing care.

Urinary Assessment

Assessment includes urinary history (pattern, symptoms, self-care ability, culture, environment), physical assessment of kidneys (costovertebral angle tenderness), bladder (palpation/percussion for distension — a full bladder is dull to percussion above the symphysis pubis), external genitalia, urethral meatus, and perineal skin. Urine characteristics: normal color is pale yellow to amber; clarity should be clear; odor is faintly aromatic. Dark concentrated urine indicates dehydration; cloudy urine with foul odor suggests infection; pink/red urine indicates hematuria. Intake and output (I&O) monitoring: output should be approximately 30 mL/hour minimum (720 mL/day) — below this is oliguria.

✅
Minimum Urine Output30 mL/hour = minimum acceptable urine output for adults. Below this = oliguria → notify provider. Anuria = <100 mL/24 hours = kidney failure. These values are consistently tested on HESI and NCLEX.

Nursing Interventions for Urinary Elimination

Health promotion: adequate fluid intake (2–3 L/day), maintain voiding habits, prevent infection (front-to-back perineal hygiene, void after intercourse, adequate hydration). Bladder retraining: scheduled voiding to gradually increase intervals; Kegel (pelvic floor) exercises for stress incontinence — instruct patient to contract perineal muscles as if stopping urine flow, hold 10 seconds, repeat 10–20 times, 3 times/day. Catheterization: straight (in-and-out) for retention or specimen; indwelling Foley for surgery, accurate output measurement, skin breakdown prevention. Safety: latex allergy check, povidone-iodine vs. chlorhexidine allergy, sterile technique required. Skin care: moisture barriers for incontinent patients — urine on skin causes maceration and increases pressure injury risk.

🧠
Female Catheterization LandmarksUrethral meatus is BETWEEN the clitoris (anterior) and vaginal opening (posterior). Clean from front to back. Use non-dominant hand to spread labia (now contaminated) — never release this hand until catheter is inserted and balloon inflated.

Chapter 47 – Bowel Elimination

▼

Scientific Knowledge Base: GI Tract

The gastrointestinal tract processes food from ingestion to defecation: Mouth (mechanical and chemical digestion begins), Esophagus (peristalsis to stomach), Stomach (churning, acid secretion, chyme formation), Small intestine (90% of nutrient absorption — duodenum, jejunum, ileum), Large intestine (water reabsorption, stool formation — ascending, transverse, descending, sigmoid colon), Rectum (stool storage until defecation), Anus (internal involuntary and external voluntary sphincters). Normal defecation involves rectal filling, involuntary internal sphincter relaxation, and voluntary external sphincter relaxation.

Factors Influencing Bowel Elimination

Multiple factors affect bowel function: Age (infants have immature sphincter control; older adults have decreased motility and muscle tone), Diet (fiber promotes motility; low-fiber → constipation), Fluid intake (dehydration hardens stool), Physical activity (promotes peristalsis), Psychological factors (stress and anxiety can cause diarrhea or constipation), Personal habits (suppressing the urge to defecate weakens the reflex), Position (squatting is physiologically ideal; bedpan use inhibits defecation), Pain (patients avoid straining), Pregnancy, surgery and anesthesia (paralytic ileus), and medications (opioids → constipation; antibiotics → diarrhea/C. diff).

🚨
Opioid-Induced ConstipationAll patients receiving opioid pain medication should receive a bowel regimen prophylactically — typically a stimulant laxative (senna). Do NOT wait for constipation to develop. Assess bowel sounds and last bowel movement before administering opioids when possible.

Common Bowel Elimination Problems

Constipation: fewer than 3 stools/week, hard dry stool, straining — treat with increased fiber, fluids, activity, stool softeners, laxatives. Fecal impaction: hard mass of stool in the rectum that cannot be passed voluntarily — oozing liquid stool around the mass (a paradox) is a key sign. Digital removal may be required. Diarrhea: loose, watery stools >3/day — risk for fluid/electrolyte imbalance and skin breakdown. Assess for C. diff (contact precautions). Bowel incontinence: inability to voluntarily control defecation — related to rectal sphincter weakness, neurological damage, severe diarrhea. Flatulence: excessive gas — ambulate early, avoid gas-producing foods, use knee-to-chest position to release gas. Hemorrhoids: dilated rectal veins — avoid straining, use sitz baths and topical treatments.

✅
Fecal Impaction — Key SignLiquid stool oozing around a hard mass = fecal impaction until proven otherwise. The patient may report feeling the urge but being unable to defecate. NEVER give a laxative without confirming impaction is not present — it will worsen the situation.

Enemas and Bowel Interventions

Cleansing enemas stimulate peristalsis by distending the bowel: tap water (hypotonic — risk for fluid overload), normal saline (safest — isotonic, can be used for infants/children), hypertonic solutions (Fleet — draw fluid into bowel, contraindicated in dehydration), soapsuds (irritates mucosa — use sparingly). Oil retention enema: lubricates and softens impacted stool; retain for 30–60 minutes. Enema position: left Sims' (lateral recumbent, left side down) — follows the natural curve of the sigmoid colon. Tube insertion: 3–4 inches for adults. Cardiac patients: obtain baseline pulse — rectal manipulation stimulates the vagus nerve and can cause bradycardia. Ostomy care: assess stoma color (healthy = pink/red, moist; dusky/dark = ischemia — report immediately), pouch emptying when 1/3–1/2 full, skin barrier protection.

🚨
Stoma Color AlertHealthy stoma = pink, moist, protrudes slightly. Dark/dusky/black = impaired circulation = EMERGENCY. Report immediately. Pale stoma may indicate anemia. Document stoma color, size, and surrounding skin condition at every assessment.
🧠
Enema Solutions — Safety SummaryNormal saline = safest for all ages. Tap water = hypotonic → fluid overload risk, avoid in infants/cardiac patients. Hypertonic (Fleet) = avoid in dehydration/renal failure. Soapsuds = irritant, use sparingly.

Chapter 48 – Skin Integrity and Wound Care

▼

Pressure Injury Staging

Stage 1: Non-blanchable erythema of intact skin — press and release; redness does NOT fade. Stage 2: Partial-thickness skin loss with exposed dermis — shallow open wound or intact/ruptured serum-filled blister. Stage 3: Full-thickness skin loss into subcutaneous tissue — fat may be visible; no exposed bone, tendon, or muscle. Stage 4: Full-thickness skin and tissue loss — exposed or directly palpable bone, tendon, or muscle. Unstageable: Full-thickness loss obscured by slough (yellow/tan/gray/green) or eschar (black/brown/tan) — cannot stage until removed. Deep Tissue Pressure Injury (DTPI): Localized non-blanchable deep red, maroon, or purple discoloration, or epidermal separation revealing a dark wound bed — may rapidly evolve to Stage 3–4.

✅
Pressure Injuries NEVER "Reverse-Stage"A healing Stage 4 does NOT become Stage 3 → Stage 2 → Stage 1. Always document using the original stage with the descriptor "healing." Example: "Healing Stage 3 pressure injury." Reverse staging is clinically and legally incorrect.

Risk Factors and Prevention

Risk factors for pressure injury development: Impaired sensory perception (cannot feel or report pain/pressure), impaired mobility, altered LOC, shear (layers of tissue slide in opposite directions — e.g., patient sliding down in bed), friction (skin rubbing against surface), moisture (incontinence maceration), poor nutrition (low albumin/protein impairs healing), impaired tissue perfusion, advanced age, and psychosocial factors. Prevention: reposition every 2 hours; pressure-redistribution mattress/surfaces; moisture barriers; adequate nutrition and hydration; minimize shear by elevating HOB no more than 30° unless medically required.

Wound Healing Phases

Hemostasis (immediate): vasoconstriction and platelet aggregation form a clot. Inflammation (1–5 days): vasodilation delivers WBCs and macrophages; signs of redness, warmth, swelling, and pain are NORMAL — do not confuse with infection. Proliferation (5 days–3 weeks): fibroblasts deposit collagen; granulation tissue forms; wound contracts; epithelization resurfaces the wound. Remodeling/Maturation (3 weeks to 2 years): collagen fibers reorganize and cross-link; tensile strength increases to a maximum of 80% of pre-injury strength — scars never fully equal original tissue strength.

Wound Healing Complications

Hemorrhage: excessive bleeding post-operatively — assess dressings frequently, mark bleeding on dressing and monitor expansion. Infection: purulent drainage, increased redness/warmth beyond wound edges, odor, fever, elevated WBC, delayed healing. Dehiscence: partial or total separation of wound edges — more common 4–5 days post-op; cover with sterile NS dressing, keep patient still, notify provider, have patient remain in low Fowler's with knees bent. Evisceration: protrusion of visceral organs through a wound opening — surgical emergency. Cover organs with sterile saline-moistened gauze, low Fowler's with knees bent, call for help STAT, NPO, prepare for emergency surgery. NEVER attempt to return organs to the cavity.

🚨
EVISCERATION = Surgical Emergency(1) Do NOT reinsert organs. (2) Cover with sterile SALINE-moistened gauze — dry gauze dehydrates and damages tissue. (3) Low Fowler's, knees bent to reduce tension. (4) Call provider STAT. (5) NPO immediately. (6) Prepare for emergency return to OR.
